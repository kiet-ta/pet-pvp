## **Story Workshop**

### _**1. Story ideation Techniques**_

**1.1 Game Type and Setting**

- Determine the genre of your game (e.g., arcade vs. role-playing) to guide the story's direction.
- Establish the setting (e.g., futuristic space or a cartoon world) to inform the narrative context.

**1.2 Character and Goal Development**

- Start with the hero's goal, which serves as the "spine" of the story, and explore the reasons behind this goal.
- Use a series of "why" questions to deepen the understanding of the hero's motivations and emotional connections.

**1.3 Antagonist and World-Building**

- Define the antagonist's goals and motivations to enrich the conflict within the story.
- Describe the game world in detail, creating a vivid backdrop that enhances the narrative and gameplay experience.

### **4. Refining the Spine**

- Creating Idea Sheets
  - Four idea sheets are used to outline key elements: the hero's quest, the hero's feelings about their goals, the antagonist's motivations, and the settings for the story.
  - The example game concept, "The Shooter," revolves around a character named Jake, who aims to reclaim his status as a top shooter after personal tragedy.

- Developing the Hero's Journey
  - Jake's motivation stems from a past accident that killed his family, leading him to prove himself in the competitive shooting league.
  - The narrative explores Jake's emotional journey and the stakes involved in his quest to win the competition.

- Introducing the Antagonist and settings
  - The antagonist, Dead-Eye Jane, is introduced as a significant challenge for Jake, representing his internal and external conflicts.
  - The settings for the game, including the League of Shooters and various competition arenas, are also developed to enhance the story's context and depth.
